Bowston: A Tail of the City's Dog Names

Article Overview

Developed in collaboration with the Ideas desk at The Boston Globe, this data-driven story analyzes more than 10,000 dog licenses across Boston to uncover how naming patterns reflect neighborhood identity, culture and lifestyle. The piece combines data analysis with interactive storytelling to explore how everyday choices reveal broader social patterns.

Key Findings

  • Luna, Charlie and Bella were the most popular names across Boston's 10,000+ licensed dogs, mirroring national naming trends
  • Local culture shows up in names, with sports references like Brady, Big Papi and Wally appearing across the city
  • Neighborhood identity shapes naming patterns: Jamaica Plain leans toward nature-inspired names like Maple and Willow, while South Boston favors classic names like Rosie and Winston
  • In areas like Mattapan, naming reflects individuality and cultural expression, with small breeds given bold names like Thor, Prince and Justice

Reporting

Analyzed the Dogs in Boston 2024 dataset using Excel and Google Sheets to clean, categorize and filter data. Built interactive visualizations using Datawrapper and Flourish, and developed the front-end using HTML, CSS and JavaScript to create an engaging, user-driven storytelling experience.

Impact

This piece uses a familiar topic to show how everyday choices reflect broader cultural and geographic dynamics across the city. It demonstrates how data can be used to create accessible, interactive storytelling that connects with a wide audience.

Publication Details

  • Platform: Northeastern University project site
  • Origin: Developed with The Boston Globe Ideas desk
  • Year: 2025
  • Type: Data-driven feature / interactive story

Whose Lanes Are These Anyway?

Overview

Reported a data-intensive story on Boston's bike lane expansion, focusing on how street design, public safety and competing stakeholder priorities intersect. Centered on Tremont Street, the project examines how infrastructure changes influence risk, behavior and access in urban spaces.

Reporting

  • Analyzed public crash and safety data using Google Sheets
  • Cleaned and structured datasets to identify trends in crashes and ridership
  • Built interactive visualizations using Datawrapper and Flourish
  • Conducted interviews with city officials and transportation advocates
  • Integrated policy context and lived experience into a cohesive narrative
  • Designed and developed the full project independently using HTML, CSS and JavaScript
  • Used AI tools (Claude, ChatGPT) to support data cleaning and workflow efficiency

Key Findings

  • Tremont Street ranked in the top 3% of Boston roadways for pedestrian crashes, with 53 EMS-reported crashes (2015–2017), including 19 pedestrian incidents and 2 fatalities
  • Citywide data shows redesigned streets saw a 51% overall decline in crashes, including 68% fewer pedestrian crashes and 57% fewer bicycle crashes
  • Infrastructure changes-lane reductions, raised crosswalks and protected bike lanes-improved visibility and reduced multi-lane risk
  • Consistent ridership across seasons suggests increased usability and perceived safety

Impact

  • Demonstrates how infrastructure design directly shapes safety outcomes and public behavior
  • Translates complex transportation data into accessible, reader-focused reporting
  • Highlights tensions between safety, accessibility and urban planning priorities
  • Combines data, reporting and interactivity into a cohesive public-interest story

Publication Details

  • Year: 2025
  • Type: Data-driven policy and public safety reporting
